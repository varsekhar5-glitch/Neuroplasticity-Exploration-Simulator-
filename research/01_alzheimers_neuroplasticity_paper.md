# The Impact of Omega-3 Fatty Acids, Aerobic Exercise, and Cognitive Stimulation Therapy on Neuroplasticity Enhancement in Early-Stage Alzheimer's Disease

*Status: in progress (seeking mentor).*

## Abstract
Alzheimer's Disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that affects memory and brain function by a progressive decline in brain function and cognition. Although it is a common disease, there is still little understanding of its causes and how to cure it. Neuroplasticity—the brain's ability to repair/heal itself in the early stage of AD—can be improved with proper lifestyle interventions and therapies. This study analyzes the impact of omega-3 fatty acids, aerobic exercise, and cognitive stimulation therapy (CST) on neuroplasticity during the early stage of AD. The study comprises literature-based reviews of clinical trials and experiments, methodology on pre-existing clinical data from mice labs, and surveys of physicians and researchers. The study investigates specific tests that mainly affect neurogenesis and executive function, as well as other key factors in AD. This research provides comprehensive insight into the importance of early intervention in Alzheimer's disease to improve diagnosis and treatment for patients.

## Introduction
Can the synergetic implementation of an omega-3 fatty acid diet, aerobic exercise, and cognitive stimulation therapy enhance neuroplasticity in early-stage Alzheimer's Disease? Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a slowly progressive disease that destroys brain cells and shrinks the human brain. The most common factor is the slow depletion of memory in individuals 65 years or older, which is why AD is the root cause of dementia. AD is "characterized by a loss of cognitive functioning—thinking, remembering, and reasoning—and behavioral abilities to such an extent that it interferes with a person's daily life and activities" (NIH–National Institute of Aging; *What is Alzheimer's?* Mar. 2025). A main region affected is the hippocampus, located in the temporal lobe, which controls learning and memory. Progression starts by attacking memory: the patient loses recent memories, then older ones, eventually all. Executive function weakens (social abilities, daily tasks, language). The true cause is unknown, but early-screening risk factors exist: amyloid plaques and tau tangles (protein build-ups consisting mainly of beta-amyloids), and the APOE4 gene, which carries lipids and cholesterol into the bloodstream and can cause a lipid-metabolism imbalance.

Current treatment is limited; there is no definite cure, only treatments that slow progression. The most common medications are cholinesterase inhibitors, primarily donepezil and memantine—they target plaques but do not prevent the effects. Holistic lifestyle treatments are also emerging: cognitive stimulation therapy, good diets, and aerobic exercise. The gap: scarce research on these treatments used synergetically.

This paper aims to enhance neuroplasticity in early-stage AD. Neuroplasticity is the brain's way of recreating/healing/strengthening itself through neurogenesis (creating new neurons). The three interventions examined: (1) **Omega-3 fatty acids** boost DHA and encourage synaptic pruning (removing old/weak synapses for stronger ones); (2) **Aerobic exercise** increases blood flow to the brain and hippocampal volume, strengthening memory storage and learning; (3) **Cognitive stimulation therapy** crystallizes cognitive abilities over time through brain-engaging activities. Hypothesis: implementing these three should measurably improve brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), hippocampal volume, and neurogenesis.

## Literature Review

### Neuroplasticity
Neuroplasticity is how the brain reorganizes its structure, adapts, and creates new neural pathways after trauma or injury. Structural neuroplasticity uses synaptic pruning (eliminating older/weaker synapses to build newer/stronger ones) and neurogenesis. Because AD is progressive, the brain cannot maintain plasticity that keeps up with the progression, causing impairments. Amyloid plaques and tau tangles cause oxidative stress—an imbalance of free radicals and antioxidants causing neurodegeneration. Amyloid-beta plaques cause impaired long-term potentiation (LTP), i.e. synaptic loss, by binding synaptic proteins, causing loss of glutamate receptors and signaling disruption. BDNF, a neurotrophin, promotes synaptic plasticity. Increased hippocampal volume correlates with memory and learning ability—the key goals of neuroplasticity.

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids
Omega-3 fatty acids (n-3 PUFA) are essential polyunsaturated fats required for inflammation regulation, cell-structure support, and healthy brain function. They support neuroplasticity in AD by enhancing neurogenesis, synaptogenesis, and brain volume. The body cannot make them, so they come from supplements and food (fish oil, fish, nuts). The two important ones studied in AD are EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid). They decrease inflammation and cognitive decline, promoting neurogenesis and synaptogenesis; DHA and EPA are crucial for neuronal membrane integrity. Individuals consuming more omega-3 in early-stage AD may mitigate the APOE4 genetic risk, with benefits more prevalent in APOE4 carriers. Long-term users showed a 64% lower risk of AD vs non-users (*The Relationship of Omega-3 Fatty Acids with Dementia and Cognitive Decline*, Apr 5 2023, NIH). In rodents, those without omega-3 showed memory and hippocampal-plasticity decline; those with it showed improvements in memory, learning, synaptogenesis, and neurogenesis. 19-month-old mice fed omega-3 for 2–18 months showed increased BDNF; 22-month-old mice had age-related spatial memory and hippocampal expression restored. 25-month-old rats given DHA for 10 weeks had ameliorated hippocampal lipid peroxidation, oxidative stress, and spatial memory. In humans, omega-3 improved brain volume in parietal, frontal, temporal, and limbic areas. EPA reduces neuroinflammation and oxidative stress, supporting BDNF growth.

### Aerobic Exercise
Aerobic exercise (cardio) uses large muscle groups to raise heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood flow to muscles and brain. For neuroplasticity, increased cerebral blood flow reduces mitochondrial dysfunction and increases hippocampal volume, VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor), BDNF, and executive function. AD patients doing 30–40 min moderate aerobic exercise 3–4×/week showed a 1–2% increase in hippocampal volume and improved memory in early AD. Moderate-to-high-intensity treadmill exercise (1 hr/day, 3 months) increased synaptic plasticity and spatial memory. Increased hippocampal volume is closely linked to BDNF growth. A 3-month moderate exercise plan increased cerebral blood flow to the anterior brain (NIH PMC, Aug 4 2023). 22-month-old mice exercising 3 min/day for 3 weeks showed increased VEGF, suggesting reduced vascular stress. Exercise raises metabolic factors (ketones, lactate, muscle-derived myokines), increasing BDNF. 12-week HIIT decreased mitochondrial fragmentation and produced a healthier mitochondrial network. Swimming 30 min/day for 4 weeks attacked amyloid plaques and tau tangles.

### Cognitive Stimulation Therapy (CST)
CST is dementia therapy designed to train the brain to become stronger and develop new neural connections through mental stimulation. It is the most implemented/researched of the three because it conditions the brain to build new synapses, memory recall, and retention. Patients undergo mentally stimulating activities (puzzles) and are encouraged to keep learning. CST can be individual (iCST) or group-based, with groups yielding higher results. A review of 238 AD patients showed generalized evidence of improved executive functioning, language comprehension/production, and quality of life (*Cognitive Stimulation Therapy for Dementia*, Alzheimer's Queensland, Aug 28 2023). An 8-week study showed greater cognitive activity in CST patients vs controls. A 12-week study showed a small memory increase (*Effectiveness of Individual Cognitive Stimulation on Cognition in Mild Alzheimer's Disease: A Multicenter RCT*, NIH PMC, Oct 26 2025). CST strengthens semantic memory, memory encoding, and free/cued recall. Limitation: small sample sizes—larger samples are needed to confirm results.

## Summary and Research Purpose Restated
Neuroplasticity is a rising factor in AD treatment and can be enhanced not just by pharmaceuticals but by lifestyle interventions that trigger vital biomarkers. The three treatments each enhance neuroplasticity via diet, fitness, and mental engagement, respectively, but are rarely used and studied synergetically. This study's goal is to discover the potential of lifestyle interventions on neuroplasticity enhancement during early-stage AD by synergetically implementing these three treatments alongside existing literature.
